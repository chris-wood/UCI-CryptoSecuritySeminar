UCI Crypto & Security Seminar

What's the point?

This is a weekly seminar in which students and faculty members present papers on various topics in cryptography, security, and privacy. The purpose is to disseminate industry and research trends and results in a friendly environment.

How can you take part?

All talks are expected to take place in DBH 4013, and are open to the UCI community; all are quite welcome to attend. If you are interested in presenting a topic, please attend one of the meetings and propose it to the group. Alternatively, you may touch base with Stanislaw Jarecki or Gene Tsudik offline. Their contact information is available on their corresponding UCI websites.

Chronological List of Seminar Talks

  • Talk: An Overview of Secure Pattern Matching

    Presenter: Sky Faber

    Abstract: The general Pattern Matching Problem has been well studied. With provably optimal solutions (on random input) given in the '70s by Knuth, Morris,Pratt, Boyer, Moore and others. In this case a single party holds both a long text T, and a pattern P. The general goal is to find all occurrences of P in T with the fewest number of single character comparisons. Recently, there has been an increased interest in Secure Pattern Matching (SPM) in which different parties hold the text and the pattern. They would like to compute all occurrences of P in T without revealing any additional information to each other about their respective inputs. In this setting, we are typically interested in measuring the number of cryptographic operations. Interestingly, to date, no provably optimal solutions exist. In this talk we discuss some of the recent advances in generic SPM, as well as some of its variants, under various adversarial models.

  • Talk: Honeywords: Making Password-Cracking Detectable

    Presenter: Gene Tsudik

    Abstract: We suggest a simple method for improving the security of hashed passwords: the maintenance of additional “honeywords” (false passwords) associated with each user’s account. An adversary who steals a file of hashed pass- words and inverts the hash function cannot tell if he has found the password or a honeyword. The attempted use of a honeyword for login sets off an alarm. An auxiliary server (the “honeychecker”) can distinguish the user pass- word from honeywords for the login routine, and will set off an alarm if a honeyword is submitted.

  • Talk: Affine-Power S-Boxes over Galois Fields with Area-Optimized Logic Implementations

    Presenter: Christopher Wood

    Abstract: Cryptographic S-boxes are fundamental in key-iterated substitution permutation network (SPN) designs for block ciphers. As a natural way for realizing Shannon's confusion and diffusion properties in cryptographic primitives through nonlinear and linear behavior, respectively, SPN designs served as the basis for the Advanced Encryption Standard and a variety of other block ciphers. In this work we present a methodology for minimizing the logic resources for n-bit affine-power S-boxes over Galois fields based on measurable security properties and finding corresponding area-efficient combinational implementations in hardware. Motivated by the potential need for new and larger S-boxes, we use our methodology to find area-optimized circuits for 8- and 16-bit S-boxes. Our methodology is capable of finding good upper bounds on the number of XOR and AND gate equivalents needed for these circuits, which can be further optimized using modern CAD tools.
